Dublin


Some History

Dublin (/ˈdʌblɪn/ ; Irish: Baile Átha Cliath) is the capital and largest city of Ireland. Situated on a bay at the mouth of the River Liffey, Dublin is in the province of Leinster and bordered to the south by the Dublin Mountains, part of the Wicklow Mountains range. As of the 2022 census, the city council area had a population of 592,713, while the greater Dublin area, including suburbs, had 1,263,219 residents, and County Dublin's population totaled 1,501,500.

Originally settled by the Gaels during or before the 7th century, Dublin later saw the arrival of the Vikings. By the 12th century, it had grown into Ireland's principal settlement following the Anglo-Norman invasion. The city experienced rapid expansion from the 17th century and became the second largest city in the British Empire after the Acts of Union in 1800. Post-independence in 1922, Dublin became the capital of the Irish Free State, later renamed Ireland in 1937. In 2018, the Globalization and World Cities Research Network (GaWC) ranked Dublin as an "Alpha minus" global city, placing it among the top thirty cities worldwide.

Book of Kells

The following was a poem plaquered on the wall at the beginning of the exhibition, and I really enjoyed it. The Book of Kells was written by St Colum Cille's followers.

St. Colum Cille the Scribe

My hand is weary with writing
My sharp quill is not steady
My slender-beaked pen juts forth
A black draught of shining dark-blue ink.

A stream of the wisdom of blessed God
Springs from my fair-brown shapely hand:
On the page it squirts its draught
Of ink of the green skinned holly

My little dripping pen travels
Across the plain of shining books
Without ceasing for the wealth of the great--
Whence my hand is weary from writing.
